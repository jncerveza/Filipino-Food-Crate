Lechon

History

The word lechon comes from the Spanish word leche, which means milk. The dish's origins can be traced back to little immature suckling piglets’ still nursing milk from their mothers. As the popularity of Lechon grew, chefs began to employ larger pigs.

Ingredients

  • 20 garlic cloves
  • 1 bunch cilantro, trimmed
  • 1 cup loosely packed fresh oregano leaves
  • 5 tablespoons kosher salt
  • 2 tablespoons black pepper
  • 2 tablespoons ground cumin
  • 1 1/2 cups olive oil, divided
  • 4 cups fresh orange juice (about 8 oranges)
  • 4 cups fresh lime juice (about 10 limes)
  • 1 (15- to 18-lb.) whole suckling pig

Procedure

  1. Place garlic, cilantro, oregano, salt, pepper, cumin, and 1/2 cup of the oil in a blender. Slowly add orange and lime juice, and pulse until smooth, 10 to 12 times. Remove 1 cup marinade mixture, and reserve for later use.

  2. Place pig in a container that is large enough to accommodate its size. Thoroughly coat with remaining marinade mixture, rubbing all over pig.

  3. Cover container, and chill 8 to 24 hours, basting occasionally.

  4. Preheat oven to 275°F. Place a wire rack in a large, aluminum foil-lined rimmed baking sheet or roasting pan. Remove pig from marinade, and place on rack, discarding marinade.

  5. Inject pig all over with reserved 1 cup marinade mixture, and rub with remaining 1 cup oil.

  6. Roast pig in preheated oven until a meat thermometer inserted into the shoulder joint close to the head registers 160°F, about 4 hours, rotating pan, if necessary.

  7. Increase oven temperature to 425°F, and roast until skin is golden brown and crispy, about 1 hour.

  8. Let rest 20 minutes. Serve immediately.
